Yunel Escobar

January 31, 2015

Yunel Escobar is a player new to the Nationals, but he is not a player new to change or trouble. In just under five seasons Escobar has been a member of six organizations including the Nats. Seemingly his talent on the field, a career 101 wRC+ and 51.5 runs saved defensively at a premium position, has been outweighed by his troubles off of it. His most notable off-field issue came in September 2012, when he was suspended for three games for wearing eye black that had a Spanish homophobic slur written on it.

This isn’t the first player the Nationals have acquired who has had trouble off the field, but they haven’t made a habit of it in reason seasons. Rafael Soriano, Bryce Harper and Jayson Werth have all developed reputations to some degree of being a jerk, but nothing to the degree of Escobar. However, during his recent time with the Rays the stories about Escobar’s personality quieted down and while he likely is not a model citizen, there is still a possibility that he can fit in to the Nationals clubhouse dynamics.

On the field he should be a valued contributor, taking over the starting second base job in 2015 and possibly shifting over to shortstop for a potentially departing Ian Desmond in 2016. And he comes at a dirt cheap price, signed to a two year contract worth just $12 million and an option for a third year at $7 million. With the price for a win above replacement valued at around $6-7 million, it’s hard to see Escobar not being worth it.

At the plate Escobar brings a different skillset than the typical Nats batter. For his career he has a healthy walk rate, at 8.9 percent, and a low strikeout rate, at 11.3 percent. Looking further Escobar doesn’t chase pitches, swinging at 22.6 percent of pitches outside of the strike zone, while making contact at a high rate, 85.2 percent. With the Nats it is more likely he’ll put up a wRC+ closer to 95 than to 121, Steamer projects him for a 98 wRC+ in 2015, but at second base that’s still above average.

Defensively, there are some bigger questions. Up until last season Escobar had great defensive numbers at shortstop, averaging 10.2 defensive runs above average in his six seasons as a starter according to FanGraphs. And then last season his numbers fell off a cliff as he was worth -11.0 defensive runs above average, second worst among all shortstops in MLB. It’s unlikely that Escobar really experienced such a sharp decline in defensive skill, but at 32 it is likely that he has experienced some decline. With a large part of his value coming from his glove, the Nats will have to hope that his 2014 was an abnormality.

Overall if Escobar can keep his off the field issues behind him and can rebound on the defensive end he should be a valuable part of the Nats for the next two or three years.
