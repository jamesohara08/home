Never Strikes Out or Walks

June 11, 2014

Today Dave Cameron at FanGraphs had his weekly chat and one user posed an interesting question as follows.

Under the time constraints of the chat, Cameron rushed into what I believe to be a weak and likely incorrect answer. I believe that such an interesting question is worthy of a little more time and investigation.

To begin with we’ll assume that the average hitter has over the course of a season 600 plate appearances. If the hitter does not walk or strikeout then we can assume every plate appearance ends with a ball in play or a home run. Let’s work with home runs first so that we can be left with just balls in play. In 2014 Major League hitters have hit 1758 home runs in 73470 plate appearances, which is a rate of .024 home runs per plate appearance. If that rate is extrapolated out over 600 plate appearances it would result in 14 home runs. So our hypothetical hitter will hit 14 home runs in his season.

Now we have the remaining 586 plate appearances to deal with. In 2014, the league average batting average on balls in play, or BABIP, is .297. So, since our hypothetical hitter is roughly league average, we’ll say he gets a hit in 29.7% of the remaining 586 plate appearances and the rest go for outs. This would give him 174 hits, not including the 14 home runs he already has. Below is a breakdown of the league average singles, doubles and triples per non-home run hit rate in 2014.

So the average hitter has about 75% of their non-home run hits fall for singles, 23% for doubles and 2% for triples. If we extrapolate those rates out over our 174 hits we get 131 singles, 39 doubles and 4 triples. Altogether this would produce a slash line of .313/.313/.462, or an OPS of .775, above the league average of .709. Or if you are more advanced stats inclined we can use Crashburn Alley’s wOBA calculator and the numbers we found above to get a wOBA of .333, again above the 2014 league average of .313. Overall, we can see that while the lack of walks hurts this hypothetical player’s on-base percentage, the gains made by not striking out are far greater. While this situation could never occur in reality, it’s still a fun idea to think about on a slow Wednesday afternoon.
